The Past, Present and Future of the Ulsoor Lake

Past

Ulsoor lake being one of the oldest lakes in Bangalore was surrounded by abundant greenery. There were in fact many islands situated in the lake. The lake was rich in flora and fauna and many species of migratory birds used to come for fish. There were also numerous species of butterflies and insects existing in the bushes near the lake. Apart from the scenic beauty, facilities for boating were also provided in the lake to attract visitors.

Present

Currently , ulsoor lake is the biggest and one among the most polluted lakes in bengaluru, has witnessed a series of issues in recent years like decrease in migratory birds as a consequence of fish killing, dumping of heavy solid waste including non-biodegradable, plastic and wet waste by residents living around the lake, inlet of untreated sewage from surrounding areas which was responsible for depletion of dissolved oxygen levels and increase in ammonia levels in water, causing repeated fish kills.

Future

Ulsoor is one among the most polluted lakes in bengaluru, due to the seepage of untreated sewage water including solid wastes (like plastics) and if this continues one day ulsoor lake will reach to a position where it will never be able to be cleaned and would froth like the rest of the polluted lakes causing varieties of diseases. Ulsoor lake would end up being called as sewage or drain in future if the flow of sewage and solids wastes continue to enter the lake.

ABOUT ULSOOR LAKE

Ulsoor Lake is one of the biggest and most beautiful lakes of the Bangalore city. It is located in the north-eastern fringe of the city, close to the eastern terminus of the M. G. Road. The lake derives its name from the locality where it is situated, namely, Ulsoor. The lake serves as a venue of the celebration of Ganesh Chaturthi festival celebrated in the month of August/September every year.

History of Ulsoor Lake

It is believed that the Ulsoor Lake was created by King Kempe Gowda II during the latter half of the second century. In the ancient times Ulsoor Lake was known as the ‘Halsur’ or ‘Alasur’ lake. However, even though the lake is dated to the time of Kempe Gowda, the present lake was created at a much later time by Sir Lewin Bentham Bowring, the then Commissioner of Bangalore. It is said that Ulsoor Lake dates back to 1537, when Kempe Gowda II founded the city and established one of his watchtowers there. Later, the lake, and the village of Ulsoor, became part of Hyder Ali’s zaagir. In 1600, the Wodeyars of Mysore bought it from the Mughals. Later the British wrested control of Bangalore from Tipu in 1798 and handed it back to the Wodeyars. After an understanding with the Wodeyars, the British began reinforcing it as a cantonment and laying railroads, building the town in a planned manner around the lake. Ulsoor was the centerpiece of the cantonment town that the British built. All the important offices, courts, hospitals, and bazaars were built around the lake. The British even created separate neighborhoods for the working class — mostly of Tamil laborers who, through their own enterprise and trade, enriched the community.

Consequences

Thousands of dead fish were washed ashore today at Ulsoor lake this part of the lake has seen heavy dumping of solid waste, including non-biodegradable plastic and wet waste, by those residing around the lake. Though Ulsoor Lake is said to be one of the better maintained lakes in the city, it also suffers from inlet of untreated sewage into the lake from surrounding areas, increasing the ammonia levels in the water. The sewage water is responsible for depleting the dissolved oxygen levels in the lake water, causing repeated fish kills. While a major portion of the dumped solid waste is bio-degradable, the nutrients seep into the water after they are broken down, which coupled with sewage, will lead to an increase in concentration of ammonia and other nutrients in the water.

Causes

The lake which has a catchment area of 1.5 km2(0.6 sq mi) is fed by three drains at different locations. A barrier was built to block the sewage water entering into the lake, the height of the barrier was short intern giving rise to the flow of sewage water into the lake. During heavy rainfall the sewage water level would rise and overflow into the lake. Solids wastes are dumped into the lake by the residents. Solid wastes include plastic, wet waste etc which are non-biodegradable vast floating layer of non-recycled debris comprising plastic bags and water bottles and even sacks.
